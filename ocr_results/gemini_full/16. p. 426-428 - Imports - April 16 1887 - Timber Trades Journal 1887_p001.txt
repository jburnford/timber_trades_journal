OBITUARY.
We have to record the sudden death of Mr. Thomas Laslett, who as the Admiralty timber inspector was well known in the hardwood trade. Mr. Laslett was the author of *Timber and Timber Trees*, a book which has for some time been recognised as the standard work on the subject. From the evidence given at the inquest held at Woolwich, before Mr. Carttar, on Saturday, it appears that the deceased gentleman was coming down the steps at Woolwich Dockyard station, when he was seen to fall, and, although assistance was promptly rendered, he shortly expired. The medical evidence showed that the heart was diseased, and the jury returned a verdict of death from natural causes.

TRADE ITEMS.
FLEETWOOD.—Messrs. Taylor, Pierce, & Co. are now discharging at this port two cargoes of flooring, most of which is going into immediate consumption.
* * *
MESSRS. ADOLPHE BOUCHE & Co., 49, Boulevard Henri IV, Paris, have established, at the above address, a company entitled "Société de Représentations Forestières," the object of which is to buy and sell in France and abroad every kind of forest product.
* * *
GREAT NORTHERN TELEGRAPH COMPANY.—The accounts of this company for the year 1886 show that after providing for a dividend per cent., and an addition of £55,555 to the reserve and renewal fund, there will be £31,586 to be carried forward to the next account.
* * *
FIRE.—Last Tuesday night the firemen at Brompton were called to a timber stack alight in Bishop's Road, Fulham, and several engines were turned out. It was found that a timber-built series of workshops were ablaze, and before the fire was subdued the workshops were gutted and fell down.
* * *
RESULT OF TENDERS.—Messrs. Geo. T. Lunt & Co., of Liverpool, have secured the contract for the supply of the timber required for the extensive alterations now being carried out at the Liverpool Exhibition, and in the erection thereat of an old Lancashire village, which will consist entirely of wood. The tender of the same firm has also been accepted for supplying the West Derby Board of Guardians with timber.
* * *
GOVERNMENT CONTRACTS.—The tenders of the following firms have been accepted for the cabinet and joiner's work required by the Admiralty:—Messrs. Brownlee & Co.; A. Blain & Son; B. Clarke, Sons, & Co., Limited; H. Best; Geo. Sims & Co.; Jas. Gamlin; Atkinson & Co.; A. Foley; E. Spencer & Co.; Chapman, Son, & Co.; J. Julian & Co., and J. Cox & Son.
* * *
MASTS FOR THE BRITISH NAVY.—Messrs. Bradley & Co., of East Saginaw, Mich., have a contract to supply the British navy with masts. They are at present getting out spars on the Pinconning branch of the Michigan Central railroad, and lately shipped a quantity to Nova Scotia, a new departure in the method of forwarding masts to the seaboard, as hitherto they have gone down the lakes by water. There must be some urgency in British naval affairs, since the builders cannot wait till the opening of navigation.
* * *
EXTENSIVE TIMBER LIMIT DEAL.—The extensive timber limits, mills and river improvements owned by Mr. Peter McLaren on the Mississippi river in the counties of Lanark, Frontenac and Addington have been purchased by Mr. W. C. Edwards, M.P. for Russell, in conjunction with some other Ottawa capitalists. The sale, which was negotiated by Mr. Andrew McCormack, of Pembroke, was finally completed recently, and the whole property, limits, meals, lumber, &c., has been formally transferred to Messrs. Edwards and Company. The area of the timber limits is about 300 square miles, and the price paid is about 900,000 dols. The saw-mills are three in number, two at Carleton Place and one on the Kingston and Pembroke Railway. —*Ottawa Free Press*.

WEATHER IN THE BALTIC.
THERE has been some snow during the week, but most of it has turned to water. Easter has set in with unusually beautiful spring weather. The ground is bare and the ice nearly gone. Next week two vessels are expected to begin loading at Sornas. On the early part of the week, when a large steamer worked its way through the ice, one may, therefore, say that navigation is open here in Helsingfors. At Abo a steamer from Hull, the *Patria*, arrived on the 6th inst., and was able without much difficulty to force the ice up to the town itself; no doubt other ports further up will follow suit very shortly.
