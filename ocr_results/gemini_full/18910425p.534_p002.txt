THE GERMAN OF IT.—A little German furniture manufacturer, while examining some poplar of 15,000 ft.—in Price, Welch, & Co.'s yard, at Baltimore, fell off the pile.
The next day while explaining the cause of his lameness, he was asked how far he fell.
"Fifteen thousand feet," he answered.
THE TELEPHONE IN NEW YORK.—In view of the agitation against high telephone charges in London it may be of interest to note that the charges of the Metropolitan Telephone and Telegraph Company of New York have been brought under the notice of the New York State Legislature.
As an exclusive owner of telephone patents in its district the company has a monopoly, but it is argued that as it asks the right to excavate, traverse, occupy, and continually disturb the highways, the public has a right to see that the rates charged shall be reasonable.
When the telephone was originally established in New York the charge was 60 dols. per year ; but when the service was bad been consolidated a monopoly the rate was advanced to 150 dols. and 180 dols.
According to sworn testimony by the officers of the company before a legislative committee in 1887, the company made a net profit of 473 per cent, within six years, or nearly 80 per cent, per annum on its original capital.
