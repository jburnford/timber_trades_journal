THE TEAK MARKET.
We extract the following from Messrs. Denny, Mott, & Dickson's Wood Market Report, dated December 3rd:—Deliveries last month from the docks in London were 1,129 loads, against 1,168 loads for the corresponding month of last year. With landings, however, of 1,811 loads, the stocks in docks still amount to the large total of 12,618 loads. As indicated in our last report, a considerable portion of this is old and inferior timber, which has been much weighed down prices, has been radically disposed of "without reserve" at public auction, realising unexpectedly good prices for this class of wood. The revival of activity in the shipbuilding industry is developing, and whilst admitting that this may be only a temporary feature, it should improve the demand for teak sufficiently to notably reduce the present admittedly heavy stocks, and terminate the disheartening losses which have been sustained by holders of this timber for so long a period. Any attempt, however, in Burmah to rush in fresh shipments before the present demand be become greatly diminished would be simply suicidal; and all tendency to check consumption on this side by suddenly "jumping" prices would be equally short-sighted. Consumers are ceasing to be masters of the situation because of sounder business; but cordial reciprocation on the part of both buyer and seller is necessary to prevent any relapse into the unhappy condition of things from which it may reasonably be thought we are at length emerging.
